### q6 — Underground discovery pathways

Confirmed primary methods (all practical for this project's toolchain):

1. **Bandcamp label page deep dives** — most reliable. Follow one label's entire catalog chronologically. Bandcamp tracks are yt-dlp downloadable directly with `yt-dlp https://artist.bandcamp.com/album/...`
2. **Discogs wants-list validation** — Discogs community "wants" count on a release correlates with collector consensus quality. A release with 200+ wants is community-validated. Browse: `discogs.com/lists` for genre-tagged essentials lists.
3. **Dirty Disco Radio tracklists** — Weekly curated show publishes full tracklists at `dirtydiscoradio.com`. Following tracklists from 3–4 episodes per genus exposes 20–30 new leads per genus.
4. **SoundCloud curator reposts** — Follow 3–5 curators per genus (label accounts, respected DJs). Their reposts surface current underground. Diminishing returns due to anti-brand shift.
5. **RA event archives** — RA tracks which artists played which nights; DJ booking histories surface artists who've played alongside known names. Useful for finding "who plays before/after X".
6. **Hype Machine** — Aggregates blog coverage, surfaces tracks that got write-ups without chart success. Search by genre tag.
7. **Volumo** — DJ-exclusive platform for high-quality, non-commercial tracks. Subscription service worth checking for undercovered genera.

### q7 — Contrarian classics vs consensus risk

Research confirmed the Matthew effect applies to music curation: popularity amplifies early popularity, disadvantaging late-bloomers. Key mitigations for this project:

- **DJ-tool tracks vs listening tracks**: Tracks valued for mixing qualities (long outro, clean structure, good loop points) often have low stream counts but high selector value. These are underrepresented in community consensus lists but essential for a radio library.
- **Regional blindspots**: UK-focused collecting (as reflected in the current library) systematically underweights US Midwest labels (FXHE, White Material), West African releases (Ostinato, Awesome Tapes), and Nordic scenes (Studio Barnhus, Music For Dreams — though Barnhus is now well-represented).
- **Catalog depth vs name recognition**: The artist "Omar-S" has one library track — but FXHE's back catalog has 50+ releases. Same with Galcher Lustwerk. Consensus coverage of these names without catalog depth is surface-level.

## Trade-Offs

| Approach | Pro | Con |
|----------|-----|-----|
| One genus at a time (deep) | Full coverage per genus, defensible quality bar | Slow; 20 genera at ~10 tracks each = 200 more research/download sessions |
| Broad sweep (2–3 tracks per genus) | Fast coverage everywhere | Surface-level; misses the community validation dimension |
| Label-first (catalog dives) | Authoritative, genre-coherent, Bandcamp-compatible | May over-index one label's aesthetic within a genus |
| Curator-first (RA/Discogs lists) | Community-validated, cross-label | Consensus bias risk; popularity bias in RA voting |

**Recommended:** Label-first for undercovered genera (Ambient, Afrobeat, Afro-Cosmic, Balearic, IDM). Curator-first for genera that already have 5+ holdings. One genus at a time, 8–12 tracks per pass, research-then-download cycle matching the existing Lo-fi House methodology.

## Quality Signal Framework (addendum 2026-04-13)

The question "how do I know this is the right track to grab?" requires a three-signal check, not a YouTube search. Every track proposal should carry these three signals — where two agree, the pick is defensible; where all three agree, it's a strong buy.

### The Three Signals

**1. Critical record** — who reviewed and canonised it
Serious editorial that evaluated the work at the time or in retrospect. Not algorithmic playlist placement.

**2. Practitioner signal** — what respected DJs actually play
What shows up in tracklists from the scene's credible selectors. 1001tracklists.com, RA podcast archives, Boiler Room set recordings.

**DJ mixtapes as practitioner signal + discovery tool:** Canonical mix recordings from key figures are a primary source of truth in their own right — not just validation but active curation. A track appearing in a genre-defining mix is one of the strongest signals that it belongs in the library. Resources:
- **1001tracklists.com** — search by track title or artist to see which DJs have played something and in which mixes
- **RA podcast archive** (ra.co/podcast) — each episode has a full tracklist; browsing by genre or DJ surfaces deep cuts that never charted
- **Boiler Room archive** — full tracklists, searchable by set
- **Mixcloud** — label and DJ mixes with full tracklists for niche scenes (Balearic, Afro-Cosmic, etc.)
- **NTS Radio archive** — particularly strong for Afrobeat, Ethnic, Global Funk, Ambient, UK Broken Beat
- Genre-defining mix series: *Fabric* (UK Techno/House), *Balance* (Melodic/Organic), *DJ-Kicks* (!K7 — cross-genre canon), *Late Night Tales* (Indie/Art Pop/Ambient)

When researching a new genus, reading the tracklist of 2–3 canonical mixes is often faster and more authoritative than any written list.

**3. Community validation** — what collectors vote with money
Discogs wants count (>100 = noted, >300 = strong, >500 = holy grail territory). Also Discogs community reviews and "have/want" ratio.

### Proposed Track Proposal Format

Every future batch proposal should follow this structure per track:

```
Artist — Track Title [Label, Year]
  Critical: RA Essential 2018 / The Wire "album of the year"
  Practitioner: Played by [DJ name] in [mix/set link] / appears in Fabric XX tracklist
  Community: Discogs — 340 wants
  Duration: 6:12 ✓
  Notes: Vinyl-only original; YouTube upload by label channel
```

Minimum bar: **two of three signals must be present** before a track is proposed. One signal alone means it's speculative. Zero signals means it's a YouTube-first pick — reject.

The exception: tracks proposed on structural grounds (good outro for mixing, key compatibility, BPM fit) can be noted as "DJ-tool pick" without community validation — but this should be flagged explicitly.
