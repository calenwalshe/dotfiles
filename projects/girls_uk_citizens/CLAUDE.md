## Research Findings (compiled 1 April 2026)

### 1. Current Fee

- **£1,214 per child** as of 9 April 2025
- **Drops to £1,000 per child from 8 April 2026** — if applications are submitted after that date, the lower fee applies
- Additional £130 ceremony fee only if the child turns 18 while the application is being processed (not applicable here)
- Total for two children at current rate: £2,428. At the post-8-April rate: £2,000.

### 2. Twins — Separate Applications Required

Each child requires their own separate MN1 application and fee. The form refers to "the child" (singular) throughout. There is no mechanism to combine siblings into a single application. Both applications were correctly started separately (confirmed by separate GWF numbers and application numbers above).

### 3. Biometrics for Children Under 10

- **Children under 5**: Photo only (no fingerprints required), though fingerprints can still be requested
- **Children aged 5-10**: Full biometrics (fingerprints + photograph) typically required
- **Both children are 9** (turning 10 on 13 April 2026) — they will need full biometrics
- Must be accompanied by a parent or legal guardian (Daniela listed as responsible adult)
- **For applicants outside the UK**: Biometrics are enrolled at a Visa Application Centre (VAC) in the applicant's country, not UKVCAS (which is UK-only). In the US, this is typically a VFS Global or TLScontact centre
- The Home Office contacts applicants with biometrics instructions after the application is submitted

### 4. Processing Times

- **Home Office service standard**: 6 months from date of application/biometrics
- **Typical range**: 8-10 weeks for straightforward cases, up to 6-8 months for complex ones
- **Worst case**: Up to 12 months for cases requiring additional checks
- Section 3(2) entitlement applications are generally straightforward since the criteria are binary (met or not met), so expect the shorter end of the range
- No priority service is available for nationality applications

### 5. Referee Requirements

**Standard requirements (Guide MN1, p.23):**
- **Referee 1**: Any nationality, must be a **professional person** (minister of religion, civil servant, accountant, solicitor, etc.)
- **Referee 2**: Must hold a **British citizen passport** AND be a professional person or over 25
- Each referee must have known the family for at least 3 years
- Neither referee can be related to the applicant, to each other, or employed by the Home Office

**Child applicant special rule:** At least one referee must have dealt with the child in a professional role (teacher, doctor, health visitor, social worker). If this cannot be provided and the attempt is documented, two referees meeting adult criteria can be accepted instead.

**Overseas applicant exception (Nationality Policy v4, p.10 — internal Home Office staff guidance):**
> "Where the applicant is living outside of the UK and does not know a British citizen passport holder who is a professional or over the age of 25, **a commonwealth citizen** or a citizen of the country in which they are residing may complete and sign the form providing they meet the other requirements and the consul considers their signature to be acceptable."

**Current referees (Vladimir Vidic and Goran Todorovic) are both Canadian citizens.** Canada is a Commonwealth country. Since the children live in the US (outside the UK), the overseas exception may allow Commonwealth citizens to serve as referees in place of British passport holders. This significantly reduces the risk — but it requires the consul to consider their signatures acceptable, so it is not a guarantee.

**Remaining concern:** Neither referee appears to have a professional relationship with the children (both are listed as "Family Friend"). The child applicant rule requires at least one professional referee. If this cannot be met, document the attempt and submit with two adult-criteria referees.

**Recommended approach:**
1. Proceed with Vladimir and Goran, relying on the overseas/Commonwealth exception
2. Ideally, find a teacher, doctor, or other professional who knows the children to serve as one referee
3. Include a note explaining that the family lives overseas and referees meeting standard British citizen requirements are not available

### 6. Document Sharing Between Applications

- **Shared parental/grandparental documents**: The Home Office does not provide explicit guidance on sharing originals between sibling applications. In practice, since the applications are processed as separate cases (possibly by different caseworkers), each application should have its own set of supporting documents.
- **Recommended approach**: Submit copies of shared documents (parent's passport, grandparent's birth certificate, residence evidence, marriage certificates) with each application. The original grandparent birth certificate can be submitted with one application with a note on the other referencing it, but this risks complications.
- **Safest approach**: Photocopy all shared documents and include a complete set with each application. Note on the covering letter that the original of the grandparent's birth certificate is included with the sibling's application (reference the GWF number).
- Per-child documents (birth certificates, passport copies, referee declarations) must be unique to each application.

### 7. Section 3(5) — The Alternative Route

**Section 3(5)** is relevant but not applicable to this family's current situation.

| | Section 3(2) | Section 3(5) |
|---|---|---|
| **When parent lived in UK** | Any 3-year period before child's birth | 3-year period ending at date of application |
| **Who must live in UK** | Parent only (historically) | Child AND both parents (currently) |
| **Grandparent requirement** | Yes — must be British otherwise than by descent | No |
| **Parental consent** | Not a statutory requirement | Mandatory, no discretion to waive |
| **Citizenship conferred** | By descent | Otherwise than by descent |
| **Key advantage** | Family can live abroad | Child can pass citizenship to own children born abroad |

Section 3(5) would give a **stronger citizenship status** (otherwise than by descent), meaning Alena and Aurora could later pass citizenship to their own children born abroad without going through this same registration process. However, it requires the children and both parents to have been living in the UK for 3 years ending on the date of application — which is not the case here.

**Strategic note from Guide MN1 (p.11)**: "If the family are living abroad, you will need to decide whether to apply under this section. Or, if there is a possibility that the family may return to live in the UK or a British Overseas Territory before the child reaches age 15, whether to wait and apply under section 3(5)." The children are currently 9, so there would be time to relocate and apply under 3(5) before they turn 18 if the family wanted the stronger citizenship status. This is a trade-off between certainty now (3(2)) and a better outcome later (3(5)).

### 8. Common Pitfalls and Reasons for Refusal

**For Section 3(2) specifically:**

1. **270-day absence limit is absolute** — there is NO discretion to waive absences exceeding 270 days during the 3-year qualifying period. Even one day over = disqualification. (The Edinburgh residence period of Sept 2011 - Dec 2015 is 4+ years, so this should be well within limits.)

2. **Wrong form** — using the wrong application form results in automatic refusal with non-refundable fees.

3. **Unmarried parents before 1 July 2006** — children born before that date to unmarried parents could only claim through the mother. Not applicable here (parents married 2003, children born 2016).

4. **Failing to prove the grandparent link** — must clearly demonstrate that the grandparent (Ross Gordon Walshe) was British citizen otherwise than by descent. His UK birth certificate proves this.

5. **Good character** — applies to children 10+. Alena turns 10 on 13 April 2026. If the application is received by the Home Office before that date, the good character requirement does not apply. **This is time-sensitive for Alena's application.** (Aurora has the same birthday, so both are affected equally.)

6. **Incomplete applications** — missing documents cause the Home Office to write back requesting them, adding months of delay. Not grounds for refusal per se, but significantly slows things down.

7. **Referee problems** — as noted above, having referees who don't meet the requirements will cause issues.

8. **Deception** — providing incorrect or fraudulent information leads to refusal and potential prosecution.

### 9. The "3 Years Before Birth" Timing

The official Guide MN1 (February 2026, p.10) states:

> "The British citizen by descent parent must have lived in the UK... for a continuous period of 3 years **at any time before the child's birth**."

This means:
- The 3 years do **NOT** need to be immediately before birth
- **Any** continuous 3-year period before the child's birth qualifies
- The parent must have been physically present in the UK at the beginning of the 3-year period
- Maximum 270 days absence during the chosen 3-year period

For this family: The Edinburgh residence (Sept 2011 - June 2015) predates the children's birth (April 2016), and a 3-year period of Sept 2011 - Sept 2014 (or any other 3-year window within the residence) qualifies. This is well-established.

### 10. After Approval — Passport Application

1. **Registration certificate** is issued upon approval. For children under 18, there is no citizenship ceremony requirement.
2. **Apply for a British passport** using the registration certificate as proof of citizenship.
3. **Child passport fees**: £58.50 online, £69.50 by post (as of 2025/2026).
4. **Processing time**: Approximately 5 weeks for a first child passport; can be longer during busy periods.
5. **Documents needed for passport**: Registration certificate, child's birth certificate, passport photos.
6. The parent with parental responsibility makes the passport application on behalf of the child.
7. **Digital status**: The child will also receive digital immigration status confirming British citizenship, viewable online.
8. **No BRP to return** — this only applies to applicants who previously held a Biometric Residence Permit (not relevant for overseas applicants).

- **Alena and Aurora turn 10 on 13 April 2026**. Applications submitted (received by Home Office) before that date avoid the good character requirement. The applications were started on 1 April 2026 — they need to be completed and formally submitted before 13 April.
- **Fee reduction on 8 April 2026**: If submission can wait until 8 April, each application costs £1,000 instead of £1,214 (saves £428 total). But this leaves only 5 days before the birthday.
- **Referee situation**: Current referees (Canadian/Commonwealth citizens) may qualify under the overseas applicant exception (Nationality Policy v4, p.10), but ideally find one professional referee (teacher/doctor) who knows the children.
